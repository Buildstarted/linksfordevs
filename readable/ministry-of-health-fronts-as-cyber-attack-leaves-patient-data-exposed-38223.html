An urgent investigation by the Ministry of Health has found several other District Health Boards and Primary Health Organisations (PHO) could have been vulnerable to a cyber attack, which exposed the personal data of up to one million patients. 

The attack on TÅ« Ora Compass's computer system also likely exposed information held by other primary health organisations: Think Hauroa, Ora Toa PHO, and Cosine PHO, the Ministry said on Saturday.

Information on as many as 913,000 patients may have been exposed by the hack.

TÅ« Ora chief executive Martin Hefford the said hacker exposed a "known vulnerability," which it was in the process of updating.

"While this was an illegal cyber attack by criminals, keeping people's information safe is our responsibility and I'm very sorry that we failed to do that."

Hefford apologised to both the patients whose information was exposed, and the GP network. 

The personal information which may have been exposed includes enrolment information including name, date of birth, address, ethnicity, and national health number, whether you are a smoker or heavy drinker, whether you are due for immunisations or cervical screening, whether someone had undergone an X-ray or ultrasound, and information on chronic conditions like diabetes, including lab results for tests like blood sugar, and prescriptions. 

It also included information about mental health, which Hefford acknowledged was "more sensitive and perhaps more distressing for people. We have information as to who has been referred for those services."  

TÅ« Ora board chair Larry Jordan said he wanted to make it very clear that no GP notes had been exposed. 

"The main message is you can still trust your doctors and your medical centre."

The breach was first identified on August 5, when the hacker 'Vanda The God' posted a picture on the homepage of the Tu Ora website. Tu Ora then referred the hack to the National Cyber Security Centre, which found there had been four attacks dating back as far as 2016.   

Two hacks appear to have been by 'hacktivists', while the other two seemed to be more sophisticated attempts to access data, Ministry of Health director-general of data and digital Shayne Hunter said. 

Since being alerted to the breach in early August, the Ministry of Health had instructed all DHBs and PHOs to report back on their systems. That report is due back in the next week. 

A hotline has been set up for people concerned about their private information: 0800 499 500.
