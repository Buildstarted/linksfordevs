My First PC

Reading time: 10-13 minutes
Posted here: 16 Jul 2020

https://cheapskatesguide.org/articles/my-first-pc.html

I bought my first PC in the Spring of 1993. It was not the first computer I bought, but it was the first useful one. Between contract engineering jobs at the time, I wanted to spend my time productively by learning the increasingly-popular C++ computer language. So, I responded to a want-ad placed in the local newspaper by an individual selling a several-year-old personal computer clone with an 80286 CPU.

Sometime in the late 1970's, the phrase "personal computer" began to supersede "microcomputer" as the generic term for a computer marketed to individuals. I and many others were annoyed when IBM hijacked "personal computer" and used it as the brand name of their computer, as if no other personal computers had existed before they created the first. Today, almost all desktop and laptop personal computers are PC clones, and no one has used the term "microcomputer" in decades.

I drove my beaten-up Toyota pickup 20 miles North to the seller's tract home in a suburb of the city. On his dining room table sat a computer in a tan rectangular metal case. In those days, computers were all about the technology, not the looks. Just about the only computers not in tan rectangular metal cases were Apple computers, and they were not much more attractive. I think the seller sold computers as a side business, because I saw a couple of them lined up against the wall. The computer that I had come to buy only had 512 KB of memory, but I explained to the seller that I needed more to run Turbo C++. So, he opened the case and added an ISA card with six more 256 KB simms of RAM. I handed him $240 in twenty-dollar bills and walked out with the computer, DOS 4.01 on 5.25 inch floppies, and a monochrome amber monitor.

In 1993 the sum of $240 seemed like a lot to pay for a computer that old. Younger readers must understand that, at a time when CPU's were doubling in power about every 18 months, a computer that was a few years old was viewed as not much more than junk. In hindsight, this was one of the best purchases I have ever made, because that PC started me down a road that changed my attitude about computers. At this point in my life, computers had become headaches that I was forced to endure in my job as an engineer. Owning a PC that I could use for my own purposes changed that paradigm for me and put me on the road to loving computers. My emotional attachment to computers is actually more complicated than simple love. Perhaps not what I would call a love/hate relationship. More like being in love with someone with bipolar disorder. Buying my first PC all those years ago put me on the path that has ultimately led to the creation of the Cheapskate's Guide website.

After I brought my PC home, I went to a local bookstore and bought a book called Object-Oriented Programming in Turbo C++ by Robert Lafore. Then, I purchased a Borland Turbo C++ compiler for $90 and went to work learning how to write C++ code. I read through the thick, Turbo C++ manual that came with the compiler and Lafore's book and tried many of the programming problems. A few months later, I was ready for the next step.

I knew the best way to learn to write code was to pick a project that would take a few months to complete. I also knew the project had to be both fun and challenging enough to motivate me to stick with it. So, I decided to write a graphical flight simulator. I immediately realized that I would have to upgrade my PC.

To give younger readers an idea of how slow computers were in those days, the ISA bus that was connected to the RAM card in my first PC ran at 8 MHz. Today's DDR4 memory runs at well over a gigahertz and makes two memory transfers per clock cycle. So, today's newer computers access RAM in the neighborhood of 300 times faster than my first PC. The fastest modern CPU's are around four orders of magnitude faster than the 286. Even the CPU in a Raspberry Pi 3 is around three orders of magnitude faster than a 286.

I had difficulty finding a 386-compatible motherboard with eight, 30-pin simm slots that would allow me to avoid buying new RAM. By the time 386 CPU's came out, the computer industry had begun transitioning from 30 and 32-pin simms to 72-pin simms. I finally found a local computer business willing to sell me a used motherboard with the simm slots I needed and a 386 SX-25 CPU for $35. The problem of shifting technologies and standards was huge, even back then, because it meant that upgrading a desktop computer not only meant replacing the CPU, motherboard, and RAM. It sometimes meant buying a new hard drive, internal cards, monitor, and other peripherals.

Not until I removed the RAM from the 286 motherboard did I discover that I had another problem. The RAM slots on the new motherboard had two banks of four simm slots each. If I could not fill the second bank of four completely, it would not work. Unfortunately the two simms that were attached directly to the 286 motherboard had actual pins soldered to their contacts that fit into holes in connectors on the 286 motherboard. Without those two simms, I would only be able to fill one bank of simm slots on the new motherboard, resulting in only half as much memory, not enough to run Turbo C++. So, I would have to de-solder the pins on those two simms before I could transfer them to slots on the new motherboard. Since I was new to computers in those days, I was not 100% sure the simms would be compatible with the 386 motherboard. Even if they were, I assumed that I would damage them in some way during the de-soldering process. So, I was shocked when the new motherboard booted up successfully and recognized all eight simms.

By today's standards, my first PC had an impossibly stingy 20 MB, 5.25" hard drive. I remember replacing it with a used 80 MB hard drive that I bought for $85 at a local store that sold mostly used computers and parts. I was happy with that purchase, because 80 megabytes of space felt so roomy compared to my old hard drive.

Walking along the isles of that particular computer store was like panning for gold. I never knew what treasures I might discover. Old and in some cases very old desktop computers lined bottom shelves all over the store. Today those old Vic-20's, Commodor 64's, TI-99/4A's, TRS-80's, TRS-80 Color Computers, and a few that were so old that I could not even identify them would be called Vintage. But in the mid 1990's, they were one step away from the land-fill, too old to be useful, even to me. Used laptop computers were stacked on eye-level shelves above the desktop computers. Large cardboard boxes filled with motherboards, sound cards, video cards, and other parts pulled from old computers occupied the bottom shelf of one isle of the store. A shelf close to the ceiling on the east wall held a row of assorted used monochrome and color CRT monitors. The wide adoption of LCD monitors by consumers was, I believe, still another five or more years away. I chose my first color VGA monitor from that shelf. Its resolution was only 640x480, but that was sufficient for my needs at the time. If I remember correctly, I paid about $100 for that monitor, which is coincidentally about what I paid for the 23" IPS monitor that I bought from Best Buy three years ago. I found an ISA video card to interface with the VGA monitor in one of the large cardboard boxes of video cards.

As my flight simulator progressed, my 386 computer quickly became unbearably slow. The graphics capability of the combination of the 386 CPU and ISA video card was not fast enough to produce more than one or two frames per second. I was forced to upgrade again. This time, I went with a Trident PCI video card and a motherboard with a 486 DX-66 CPU, which forced me to buy new 72-pin RAM.

I spent a total of six months writing my flight simulator during two periods of unemployment. It went surprising well, and I learned much about C++ coding, computer memory models, video memory, and hardware interrupts. Soon, I found myself looking for intermediate-level C++ books, and not finding many.

To make a long story short, I kept the case and power supply of my first PC for 13 years as I upgraded through 4 motherboards, DOS 5.0, DOS 6.11, Windows 95, and Windows 98. Eventually, I grew tired of lugging the heavy case around the country as I moved from job to job, and I let go of the case and power supply. However, I held on to the original 8, 256 KB simms of RAM. You can see them in the picture at the top of this article. Sometimes, I wish I still had that case and power supply for nostalgic reasons, but as with most material things that have outlived their usefulness, I could not justify keeping them around. Luckily, websites have no weight and never outlive their usefulness, because they are infinitely upgradable.
