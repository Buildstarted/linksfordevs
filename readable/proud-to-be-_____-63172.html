Proud to be |_____|

by https://medium.com/@martin_29969
Reading time: 6-7 minutes
Posted here: 20 Jun 2020

https://medium.com/@martin_29969/proud-to-be-e19375a0bd89

If someone you knew exclaimed “I’m proud to be French!”, what would be your first thought? The Louvre? French cuisine? The Eiffel Tower perhaps?

What if that same person said they were proud to be Polish? What might you think then? Sauerkraut, endless recipes involving potatoes, or perhaps The Witcher book series?

How about German culture? My picks would be savory beer, bratwursts, expansive cathedrals perhaps. I imagine car enthusiasts would immediately picture the BMW or Mercedes brands.

Last one, I promise: What if someone you know exclaims “I’m proud to be White!”? What comes to mind then?

People are posting and reposting articles on social media about this topic, and I found one in particular that captured my fancy. In it, the anonymous author airs a laundry list of grievances about how White people can’t have or do anything that celebrates their Whiteness for fear it would be labeled racist. It highlighted the existence of “Black-only” things like the NAACP, BET, the United Negro College Fund, and how White people can’t have “White-only versions” of these things because that would be “racist.”

The author carries on with more examples: the Miss America beauty pageant, and how anyone can register to enter, but only black participants can enter into the Miss Black America Pageant, and how Black officers can arrest White citizens without being accused of racism, but not the other way around. After several more examples, the author wraps up their article by suggesting rhetorically that it seems only White people can be accused of being racist, and that they aren’t allowed to be proud of their skin tone. In a final stroke of the pen, the author suggests that everything above is a sign of decline for White people’s rights and privileges going back decades.

Laboring under the presumption of positive intent (a difficult task, I must confess), the author triggered a few questions in my mind: What and where is the country of origin for White people, and what kind of food originates from there? What is their official language, and are they a democratic republic like the United States?

My questions seem rhetorical, but hear me out: what comes to mind when you hear exclamations of White pride? What do you think the author is trying to convey to you?

As a product of America’s public education system, a few examples come to my mind: the annexation of land belonging to Native Americans, the Slave Triangle, and Colonialism. In fact, the longer I dwell on the term “Whiteness”, the more things come to mind: Poll Taxes, Juneteenth, and the Tuskegee Experiment. Strangely enough, I can’t think of any positive examples by a group that felt their Whiteness was key to their success. If we had the chance to travel back in time and ask the Wright Brothers the reason for their success, do you think they would say it was preordained because of their skin attributes?

You know Whataboutism has reached a stunning precipice when someone holds up historically significant minority-focused institutions as justification for a return to Separate But Equal. It has me wondering: Did the author sleep through that part of U.S. History in their education, or does it simply hit differently for privileged students than students of color?

I’m reminded of a scene from Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone that perfectly captures this gluttony: to set the stage, it’s Dudley Dursley’s eleventh birthday, and he asks his mother how many presents he gets to open this year, to which his mother replies thirty-six. Dudley reacts rather poorly to this new information, descending into a fit of rage because he remembered getting thirty-seven presents the year prior! Mind you, as this scene plays out, Harry Potter (who is lucky to even get table scraps for dinner every night) is looking onward as if watching Groundhog Day on repeat.

When I hear phrases like “embrace your Whiteness”, I have to wonder: does it encourage people to say “not my problem” when a black man shouts “I can’t breathe!”? Does it mean that a shoulder shrug is an appropriate response to a video of an unarmed black man being shot in the back? If it was Harry’s birthday, and he asked for one shiny gift-wrapped present with a bow on top, I think we all can imagine Harry’s uncle, Vernon, shrugging his shoulders and saying “Not my problem!” This is what willful ignorance, fueled by hatred, looks like.

All colors and creeds have skeletons in their closet, to be sure. While the subject of Human Rights abuse isn’t confined just to those who embrace their whiteness, what makes White Pride troubling is its obsession with attributes of physical appearance, and no other markers of community like a place, language, or a shared history. It doesn’t evoke a love for the origin of hot dogs (Germany), Post-Impressionism Art (France), or Classical Music (all of Europe, really), nor does it harken back to the glory days of battles past, or key moments of positivity and inclusivity in history. It is simply Whataboutism in its most venomous form: an invitation to deny a shared history of systemic oppression (and the role hatred plays in it), and double down on the perverted pride of not being Black. Instead of exerting pride in actual cultures and events, it suggests that radical elements like the KKK, and the White Pride World Wide initiative are not that bad really, and Its only purpose is to maintain the ignorance of law & order, amidst the backdrop of fathers, brothers, and sons shouting “I can’t breathe!”
