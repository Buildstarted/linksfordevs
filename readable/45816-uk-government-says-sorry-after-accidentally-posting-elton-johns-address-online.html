UK Government says sorry after accidentally posting Elton John's address online

Updated December 29, 2019 16:23:27

The British Government has apologised to Sir Elton John and other people given awards in the New Year honours list, after inadvertently posting their addresses online.

Key points:

  • The addresses were included in a spreadsheet when the Cabinet Office uploaded the list of recipients
  • Sir Elton John and Olivia Newton-John were among those on the honours list
  • The Cabinet Office said the addresses had been taken down from the website "as soon as possible"

The first version of the honours list contained the home or work addresses of more than 1,000 recipients, including singer Sir Elton John, and was visible for around an hour, according to the BBC.

The Cabinet Office said the addresses had been published in error and taken down from the website "as soon as possible".

"We apologise to all those affected and are looking into how this happened," the Cabinet Office said.

The list reportedly included the addresses of prominent British politicians and senior police officers, as well as figures from the entertainment and sporting worlds.

British-born Australian singer and actress Olivia Newton-John was made a dame, with Sir Elton, who was knighted in 1998, receiving the highest acknowledgement, the Companion of Honour, for his services to music.

British-born Australian singer and actress Newton-John, 71, is best known for the 1978 musical Grease in which she sang the duet You're The One That I Want with John Travolta.

Honours also went to Ashes hero Ben Stokes, his captain Joe Root and footballer Jill Scott.

Australian Trevor Bayliss, 57, the former England cricket head coach, received an OBE.

Among those to be knighted were film directors Sam Mendes and Steve McQueen, and cricketer Clive Lloyd.

Mendes, 54, directed the Skyfall and Spectre James Bond movies while McQueen, 50, won the 2014 Best Picture Oscar for his film 12 Years a Slave.

Lloyd, 75, was a middle-order batsman and captain of the West Indies between 1974 and 1985 at a time when the team reached the peak of its powers.

The Cabinet Office confirmed it had reported the matter to the ICO (Information Commissioner's Office) and was contacting the people affected.

The ICO, which has the power to investigate and fine organisations for data breaches, said it was looking into the matter.

Under strict new rules known as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which came into force in May 2018, the watchdog can dish out fines of up to 17 million pounds ($32 million), or four per cent of an organisation's turnover.
