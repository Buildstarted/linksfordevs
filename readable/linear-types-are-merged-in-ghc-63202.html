How we got there

Inception

The journey towards linear types in GHC started in the fall of 2016. At this point in time, Jean-Philippe Bernardy and I had been working on distributed storage R&D for a year or so. In the fall of 2016, together with my colleague Mathieu Boespflug, we brainstormed for a couple of months, refined our design. Proposal

The prototype only modified the front end of GHC and didn‚Äôt touch Core yet. Yet we felt confident enough to write a proposal for extending GHC with linear types in November 2017. While the proposal was being hotly debated, we carried on with the implementation. An implementation doesn‚Äôt lie: we started it to find out what we might have missed in our design. In the process, Matthew found bugs in our backward compatibility story. And so we updated our design, and the proposal.

In October 2018, my colleague Krzysztof Gogolewski joined the project. And glitches were found. In the course of this implementation, a lot of research had happened: it turns out that teaching linear types to Core is not as simple as just implementing linear logic, or even the calculus from our article, but this is a story for another time. We addressed the glitches by January 2020. The rest of the year has been focused on hunting performance regressions.

In GHC 8.12

Linear base

When GHC 8.12 is released, we will release the first version of linear-base, a toolkit to get you started with linear types. It contains linearised versions of many functions from the Haskell base library, two kinds of functors, mutable data structures with pure API, a monad for safe management of resources, linear optics, an API for allocation-free array pipelines, ‚Ä¶

The linear-base library is currently being developed by my colleague Divesh Otwani, and previously by Bhavik Mehta, during his summer internship at Tweag.

Evidently, we still have a lot of work ahead of us to make linear types in GHC a smooth experience. Completing the design of the proposal, and, probably, going beyond the proposal.
