Queensland eases restrictions early: Here's what's changing

by https://www.abc.net.au/news/stephanie-zillman/6948710
Reading time: 4-5 minutes
Posted here: 31 May 2020

https://www.abc.net.au/news/2020-05-31/what-does-coronavirus-queensland-restrictions-easing-mean-for-me/12303370

From midday on Monday, Queenslanders will be able to enjoy unrestricted travel throughout the state.

The State Government said the move effectively made Queensland its own "travel bubble" and would be a lifeline for the tourism sector.

It will also be a move welcomed by the airline industry, with intra-state flights set to resume.

Premier Annastacia Palaszczuk said she had been able to bring forward intra-state travel because of so few coronavirus cases, but also because of the detailed plans submitted by the tourism industry.

"I've had many requests from people, wanting to know when they can go visit their mum in Maryborough, or their father out in Longreach," she said.

"Well, from noon … Queenslanders can travel anywhere in Queensland," she said, adding that people can stay overnight.

"If you have a camper van and you want to hitch up your trailer and get going to the outback, now is your opportunity.

"There's no better time to jump in the car, go and visit that place that you've never visited before, take that once-in-a-lifetime trip."

Travel restrictions remain in place for people entering Indigenous communities, which remain protected zones under the Biosecurity Act.

Deputy Premier and Health Minister Steven Miles said a roadmap to lifting restrictions in those communities was being worked on with community leaders, and was made more complicated by intersecting state and federal laws.

Mr Miles also said health services had been encouraged to resume all elective surgeries and procedures.

"We hope to clear those waiting lists as quickly as we possibly can," Mr Miles said.

The other major change is the number of patrons allowed into venues.

What exactly is changing?

From June 1, 20 people be allowed to dine-in at restaurants and cafes, to attend a gym, cinema, library, museum, art gallery, or place of worship.

The same rule will apply to bars, but patrons must be seated.

Venues are required to have "COVID-safe" plans in place, and in the majority of cases, these have been submitted by the sector's peak body.

Only venues that have unique services — such as Australia Zoo — are required to submit a bespoke COVID-safe plan.

In announcing the changes, Chief Health Officer Dr Jeannette Young reminded people all of the same rules around keeping a 1.5-metre personal distance, washing hands regularly, and staying home if you are sick, apply.

Importantly, Dr Young said venues were also responsible for keeping the contact details of anyone who attended their premises, should it be needed for contact tracing in the event of an outbreak.

"I know it's onerous, but we are asking people to keep that documentation for eight weeks," she said.

Any other changes coming?

From June 5, hospitality venues will be allowed to have 20 people per "section" of their establishment.

This means at a pub with multiple restaurants and rooms, up to 20 people could be accommodated in each room, as long as there are no more than one person per 4 square metres.

"For example, some surf clubs, pubs and RSLs may have four or five defined separate indoor and outdoor areas — they can have 20 people in each of these areas if the areas are big enough," Deputy Premier and Health Minister Steven Miles said.

Mr Miles said very large venues could also erect temporary partitions to allow them to host groups of 20.

The same rule would apply for gyms, meaning they could reopen with up to 20 people in each section.
