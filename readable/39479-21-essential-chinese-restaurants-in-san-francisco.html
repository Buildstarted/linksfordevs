21 Essential Chinese Restaurants in San Francisco

View as Map

The history of Chinese food in San Francisco goes back for about as long as there has been Chinese food in the US — back to the days of the gold rush, the intercontinental railroad, and, of course, the start of the country’s oldest (and still vibrant) Chinatown. These days, San Francisco is still home to the kind of robust dim sum scene that befits its Cantonese roots, but over the years the city has also seen a burgeoning of China’s other regional cuisines, especially out west in the Richmond and Sunset neighborhoods. Eat your way through the 21 restaurants on this greatest hits list — spanning hot pot, soup dumplings, red-cooked pork belly, and more — for a taste of why SF is still one of America’s preeminent Chinese food cities.

More in the mood for sushi, burgers, ramen, or sweets? Let our other curated maps point you in the right direction.

Often described as a kind of soulful cousin to Cantonese food, the cuisine of China’s migratory Hakka people abounds with pickled vegetables and slow-cooked meats. This homey Outer Richmond restaurant is a great place to get hooked on these rustic, comforting flavors. Must-order dishes include the salt-baked chicken, clams stir-fried with basil, and braised pork belly with preserved mustard greens.

2. Old Mandarin Islamic Restaurant 老北京

On cold nights, many of the regulars who crowd the small, scruffy dining room at Old Mandarin Islamic will order one of the Beijing-style hot pots, served plenty spicy for those who request it. In general, the restaurant, which specializes in Chinese Muslim cuisine, is a great place to feast on lamb — stir-fried with cumin, braised, or boiled in a claypot. Chili-heads will want to test their endurance against the side dish/condiment known as la si ni, or “spice you to death.”

3. Shanghai House Restaurant 家家福

San Francisco is gifted with an abundance of solid Shanghainese restaurants, and Shanghai House is one of the most consistently enjoyable, known for its steamers of thin-skinned, appropriately broth-laden soup dumplings. Come, too, for a classic Shanghainese breakfast on weekends, including one of the city’s best renditions of salty soy milk, or xian dou jiang — perhaps the only version in SF that incorporates the pungent dried baby shrimp that Chinese and Taiwanese expats crave.

4. Jiangnan Cuisine 江南蘇味館

As its name indicates, this Outer Richmond restaurant specializes in the food of Jiangnan — or, to be specific, the sweet but balanced flavors of Wuxi, a city north of Shanghai. Try the show-stopping stewed duck, which comes covered in pitch-black, syrupy sauce, or the best-in-class lion’s head meatball. Better yet, go for the fatty red-braised pork belly — perhaps the most tender, most delicious version in town.

5. Terra Cotta Warrior 老家陕西

Head to this Outer Sunset gem for Shaanxi cuisine, which many Americans became acquainted with thanks to Xian Famous Foods in New York. The menu’s highlights include lots of lamb dishes; stretchy, semi-translucent cold noodles (or mian pi); Shaanxi-style pita bread; and the meat-stuffed sandwiches known as “Chinese burgers” — all served in a comfortable, classy setting.

Perhaps San Francisco’s reigning queen of fancy, innovative dim sum, Dragon Beaux boasts weekend morning lines to rival the ones at its esteemed sister restaurant, the original Koi Palace in Daly City. It’s hard to think of a local dim sum house that serves a better classic siu mai, but make sure to also save stomach space for the restaurant’s more modern offerings: sugar-crust-topped baked pork buns, bright-red rice crepe rolls stuffed with batter-fried fish, and a mushroom-and-chicken bao made to look like an actual mushroom.

8. Dumpling Specialist 嵐翔生煎饅頭

Run by the chef who used to operate Dumpling Kitchen a few blocks down on Taraval, this pint-sized restaurant’s most popular dish is actually the juicy, pan-fried sheng jian bao, a plate of which you’ll see at nearly every table. Sure, there are dumplings aplenty, in various permutations, but the real finds are the northern Chinese breakfast specialties: fan tuan, or sticky rice rolls, and perhaps the city’s richest, creamiest version of salty soy milk (xian dou jiang). Best of all, these items are available whenever the restaurant is open, even during dinner hours.

9. House of Pancakes 餡餅之家

Way more exciting than IHOP, this no-frills northern Chinese spot in Parkside serves up flaky scallion- and sesame seed–flecked pancakes, often rolled with up with thinly sliced beef or scrambled eggs. It’s a carb-fest here: The dumplings and hand-pulled noodles are also pretty great.

This Inner Mongolian hot pot restaurant offers a variety of standard soup bases, but the best play is to splurge on the Inner Mongolian lamb pot — even a half-order of which, along with a couple of a la carte meats and vegetables, will easily feed three or four. The treasures here are the lamb marrow bones and tenderloin bones, which, over the course of a communal hot pot meal, infuse the soup with so much meaty, soul-warming flavor. Don’t forget to hit the dipping-sauce station, where diners can improvise their own condiments. Make one with a sesame paste base; load another up with fermented tofu.

11. Beijing Restaurant 北京小館

How lucky San Francisco is to have a Beijing restaurant whose whole reason for being isn’t its Peking duck. Instead, regulars at this Excelsior mainstay mostly come for the kitchen’s northern Chinese dough-based dishes: chewy hand-made noodles; big, Beijing-style crepes and pancakes; and little stir-fried flour balls.

12. Hing Lung Company 興隆燒臘肉食公司

Fine, Hing Lung barely qualifies as a “restaurant,” with its cramped quarters and total lack of seating. But the crackly-skinned roast pig and succulent, honey-brushed char siu make for some of most luxurious bites in the whole city, to say nothing of Chinatown. This is a Cantonese barbecue shop, plain and simple, with only a few steam-table offerings (like braised chicken feet and fried chicken wings) to supplement the main event: roasted meats so good, it’d be a shame not to pull out a few pieces to munch on before hitting the road.

13. Eight Tables by George Chen 陈家八桌

The crowd-pleasing, something-for-everyone Market Restaurant on the first floor of George Chen’s mammoth, $20 million-plus China Live complex might seem like the more obvious pick, but Eight Tables, the swanky supper club upstairs is the more ground-breaking spot for SF: a restaurant that leans into the luxuriousness of traditional high-end Chinese dining while also translating the experience for a Western diner. At $200+ a pop, the tasting menu format obviously isn’t going to be everyone’s cup of tea, but the very best dishes — like the nine-item opening salvo of cold plates — are simply stunning.

While it shares a couple of dishes in common with sister establishment Mister Jiu’s downstairs, Moongate Lounge is very much its own thing: more cocktail lounge than restaurant, more snacky hangout spot than place to tuck into a proper entrée. Setting aside the sexy-times vibe, dim lighting, and quite excellent cocktails, the kitchen’s remixed, California-Cantonese takes on classic dim sum are worth a trip on their own. Don’t miss the prawn toast — the best deal on the menu — or the exceedingly fun, exceedingly tasty “chicken in a space suit.”

SF native Brandon Jew’s reboot of the old Four Seas banquet hall attempts to bridge the gap between Chinatown locals and outsiders who’d like to get to know the neighborhood and its cuisine. The dining room is probably the most beautiful space in Chinatown, with sweeping views to match the intimidatingly attractive clientele. Most importantly, Jew’s food is fairly one-of-a-kind in its intermixing of Californian and old-school Cantonese traditions, from the chef’s Dutch Crunch barbecue pork buns to his salt-baked local trout.

This is the spot to seek out in Chinatown when a craving for tongue-numbingly spicy Sichuan fare hits — even President Obama couldn’t resist paying a visit when he was in town. Favorites include Sichuan standards like chilled beef tendon, chicken with explosive chili pepper, and one of the better versions of wontons in red chili oil around.

17. Bund Shanghai Restaurant 上海飯店

Everyone orders the xiao long bao, as you do in a Shanghainese restaurant, and few diners will feel sorry about devouring a steamer’s worth of Bund Shanghai’s very respectable version. The tender lion’s head meatball is another favorite here, as is the jiggly red-braised Dongpo pork belly. The fact that the latter is available in a convenient single-serving portion is one of the Chinatown lunch scene’s best-kept secrets.

18. Sam Wo Restaurant 三和粥粉麺

This iconic, century-old restaurant is now open in a new spot with much of the same food, but in a brighter space with an easier-to-peruse menu. Late nights on the weekend, the crowd is nothing if not eclectic — a mix of bar hoppers, out-of-towners, and off-shift Chinatown cooks. A visit isn’t complete without an order of the fish jook and the barbecue pork rice rolls, but for something unique, try Sam Wo’s famous raw-fish salad, which comes loaded with preserved vegetables and candied citrus rinds.

Whatever you do, don't skip out on the salt-and-pepper Dungeness crab, an aggressively seasoned, deep-fried version of the local delicacy that is the restaurant's most well-known dish. Of course, there’s plenty of other great, elegant Cantonese fare at this bustling, three-story restaurant — dishes to please seafood lovers as well as connoisseur’s of Chinese-style roasted poultry. Children and Westerners, in particular, go wild for the sweet, tender “Special Beef.”

  • Foursquare

20. Harborview Restaurant & Bar 凱悅匯

Run by members of the R&G Lounge crew, Harborview is more than just a swanky banquet space with nice views of the water. It’s also serving some of the tastiest dim sum in the city, turning out consistently solid versions of all the standards, from har gow to siu mai to baked barbecue pork buns. For diners who are feeling a little bit fancy, the roast squab is a worthwhile splurge.

Sure, it's expensive for dim sum, but you mostly get what you pay for at Yank Sing, whether you’re talking about the egg tarts or the scallop dumplings. Even the vegetarian dumplings are a cut above. And, despite the fact that it is definitively not a Shanghainese restaurant, the xiao long bao are surprisingly sublime — maybe better than any other version available in the city.
